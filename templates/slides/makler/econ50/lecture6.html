"Smartphones Don't Make Us Dumb"

The New York Times, January 21, 2015

AS much as we love our digital devices, many of us have an uneasy sense
that they are destroying our attention spans...
Our ability to concentrate is shot, right?

Research shows that our intuition is wrong. We can focus.
[But] paying attention requires not just ability but desire.
Technology may snuff out our desire to focus.

...Attention is so central to our ability to think that a significant deterioration
would require a retrofitting of other cognitive functions.
Mental reorganization at that scale happens over evolutionary time,
not because you got a smartphone.

...The digital world carries the promise of amusement that is constant, immediate and limitless. If a YouTube video isn’t funny in the first 10 seconds, why watch when I can instantly seek something better on BuzzFeed or Spotify? The Internet hasn’t shortened my attention span, but it has fixed a persistent thought in the back of my mind: Isn’t there something better to do than what I’m doing?

Apply the tools of consumer theory...

"Technology hasn't changed our ability to focus,
but our desire to focus."

"Ability" = budget set

"Desire" = preference ranking

"The Internet hasn't shortened my attention span,
but it makes it easier to switch my focus
from Youtube to Buzzfeed to Spotify."

"Easier to switch" = price decrease

(doesn't require change in preferences)

Quantitative Comparative Statics: Elasticities of Demand

(Own) Price Elasticity

Inelastic if |\epsilon_{x^*,P_x}| < 1

Unit elastic if |\epsilon_{x^*,P_x}| = 1

Elastic if |\epsilon_{x^*,P_x}| > 1

Cross Price Elasticity

Substitutes if \epsilon_{x^*,P_y} > 0

Complements if \epsilon_{x^*,P_y} < 0

Neither if \epsilon_{x^*,P_y} = 0

Income Elasticity

Normal if \epsilon_{x^*,I} > 0

Inferior if \epsilon_{x^*,I} < 0

Comparative Statics: Cobb-Douglas

REMEMBER: COBB-DOUGLAS DEMAND

For the Cobb-Douglas utility function u(x,y) = cx^ay^b

the consumer will spend fraction \displaystyle{\frac{a}{a+b}} of her income on X

and the remaining fraction \displaystyle{\frac{b}{a+b}} of her income on Y:

\displaystyle{x^* = \left(\frac{a}{a+b}\right)\frac{I}{P_x}}

\displaystyle{y^* = \left(\frac{b}{a+b}\right)\frac{I}{P_y}}

Let's go back to high school

For a continuously differentiable function, we find the (unconstrained) maxima and minima by setting the first derivative equal to zero.

...but not every function is continuously differentiable, and sometimes constraints get in the way.

Suppose y = 9 - (x + 1)^2.

What is the maximum value of y if x \ge 0?

9 8 2 0

Examples where calculus

(specifically, taking the derivative and setting it equal to zero)

won't find the maximum for x \ge 0:

Example Why Calculus Fails
y = 9 - (x + 1)^2 Derivative equal to zero when x < 0;
maximum occurs at the nearest "corner" of the constrained set.
y = 9 - x Derivative never equal to zero (the function is always decreasing);
maximum occurs at the lowest "corner" of the constrained set.
y = 9 - \left|x - 1\right| Derivative doesn't exist at the maximum; solution occurs at an interior "kink."
y = 9 + (x-1)^2 Derivative equal to zero at the minimum, not maximum.

Perfect Substitutes

We are perfectly willing to substitute one good for another (not always in 1:1 ratio)

Examples: 1 Coke or 1 Pepsi, 1 Dime or 2 Nickels

The rate at which we're willing to exchange one for the other is constant.

Functional form: u(x,y) = ax + by

Suppose u(x,y) = 2x + y.

What will the optimal bundle of X and Y be?

Spend 1/3 of your income on X,
and 2/3 of your income on Y. Spend 2/3 of your income on X,
and 1/3 of your income on Y. Buy only X if P_x < 2P_y; otherwise buy only Y. Buy only X if P_x < \frac{1}{2}P_y; otherwise buy only Y. Just buy whichever one is cheaper!
They're perfect substitutes, right?

Some simple numbers

u(x,y) = 2x + y

P_x = 3

P_y = 2

I = 12

With your $12, you could buy 4 units of X, or 6 units of Y.

You like X twice as much as Y, so 4 units of X is worth more to you than 6 units of Y.

Put another way: every unit of X you get costs you 1.5 units of Y \left(\frac{P_x}{P_y} = 1.5\right);
and every unit of X gives you 2 times as much utility as a unit of Y (MRS_{x,y} = 2),
you're always willing to exchange 1.5 units of Y for 1 unit of X.

For the perfect-substitute utility function u(x,y) = ax + by

the consumer will spend all her income on Y if \frac{a}{b} < \frac{P_x}{P_y},

Perfect Complements

We always want to consume goods together

in a particular proportion

Examples: 2 creams: 1 coffee, 1 left shoe: 1 right shoe

Having additional units of either, beyond our desired ratio, does not raise our utility.

Functional form: u(x,y) = \min(ax,by)

Suppose u(x,y) = \min(2x,y).

What will the optimal bundle of X and Y be?

Buy twice as much X as Y. Buy twice as much Y as X. Buy only X if P_x < 2P_y; otherwise buy only Y. Buy only X if P_x < \frac{1}{2}P_y; otherwise buy only Y. Just buy pairs of X and Y!
They're perfect complements, right?

Some simple logic

u(x,y) = \min(2x,y)

P_x = 3

P_y = 2

I = 14

You want to set 2x = y; otherwise you're buying too much of one or the other.

You can think of this as buying "bundles" of one unit of X, and two units of Y.

Each of these bundles costs P_x + 2P_y = \$7.

With your $14, you can buy two of these bundles, for a total of 2 units of X and 4 units of Y.

DEMAND FOR PERFECT COMPLEMENTS

For the perfect-complements utility function

u(x,y) = \min(ax,by)

the consumer will buy as many bundles of

(b units of X, a units of Y)

as she can afford. Since each bundle costs bP_x + aP_y:

\displaystyle{x^* = b\left(\frac{I}{bP_x + aP_y}\right)}

\displaystyle{y^* = a\left(\frac{I}{bP_x + aP_y}\right)}

Quasilinear Utility

One good has diminishing marginal utility,

the other good has constant marginal utility.

Example: one good vs. a "composite good"

Functional form: u(x,y) = v(x) + y

Depending on v(x),
indifference curves might cross the axes.

The general approach:

Marginal utilities are positive, and indifference curves are smooth and convex in shape.

When the "normal method" yields positive quantities, great.

When the "normal method" yields a negative quantity, go to the closest corner (purchasing zero of the good that would have been negative at the tangency).

This allows us to model goods that you buy none of
when their price is too high or income is too low (reasonable!)

Example: u(x,y) = a \ln x + by

MRS_{x,y} = \frac{a}{bx}

Setting this equal to \frac{P_x}{P_y} yields the tangency condition:

P_xx = \frac{a}{b}P_y \Rightarrow x^* = \frac{aP_y}{bP_x} \text{ (always positive; affordable if }I \ge \frac{a}{b}P_y)

Plugging this into the budget condition yields

\frac{a}{b}P_y + P_yy = I

y^* = \frac{I}{P_y} - \frac{a}{b} \text{ (negative if }I < \frac{a}{b}P_y)

DEMAND FOR QUASILINEAR UTILITY

u(x,y) = a \ln x + by

Case 1: I > \frac{a}{b}P_y

x^* = \frac{aP_y}{bP_x}

y^* = \frac{I}{P_y} - \frac{a}{b}

(Interior Solution)

Case 1: I \le \frac{a}{b}P_y

x^* = \frac{I}{P_x}

y^* = 0

(Corner solution)

Summary: Kinks and Corners

Know the conditions under which calculus doesn't "work" to find an optimum.

Solve situations with kinks and/or corners with logic and common sense.

Even when tangency conditions won't determine the optimum,
comparing MRS to the price ratio is key to our analysis.

Summary: Deriving and Analyzing Demand

Memorize and understand the demand for the three "canonical" functional forms:
Cobb-Douglas, Perfect Complements, Perfect Substitutes.

Know how to derive the demand for new functional forms. [Midterm alert!]

Know how to demonstrate comparative statics
visually (price consumption curve, income consumption curve, Engel Curve),
quantitatively (own-price, cross-price, and income elasticities),
and verbally (complements/substitutes/neither, normal/inferior)
