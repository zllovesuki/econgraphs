Homogeneity of Degree Zero

A function f(x,y) is homogeneous of degree zero if f(x,y) = f(tx,ty) for any nonzero t.

The conditional demand function must yield the same value for L when all w and r increase by the same factor.

In other words, the conditional labor demand
must depend only on the ratio \frac{r}{w}.

Market Demand and Supply

Suppose there are N_C consumers and N_F firms.

Individual Market, if Identical Market, if Different
Demand \displaystyle{q^D(P)} \displaystyle{Q^D(P) = N_C \times q^D(P)} \displaystyle{Q^D(P) = \sum_{i=1}^{N_C}q^D_i(P)}
Supply \displaystyle{q^S(P)} \displaystyle{Q^S(P) = N_F \times q^S(P)} \displaystyle{Q^S(P) = \sum_{j=1}^{N_F}q^S_j(P)}

Short-Run Competitive Equilibrium

Number of consumers and firms is fixed

Capital is fixed

Wages, other prices, preferences might fluctuate

Prices adjust so that Q^S(P) = Q^D(P)

Firm and Market Supply: 100 Firms

Given: u(x,y), f(L,K), P_y, I, w, r, \overline{K}, N_C, N_F.

Goal: equilibribium price and quantity of X

1. Derive individual consumer demand for X, q^D(P_x), as a function of P_x, P_y, I
using the tools of consumer theory.
2. Multiply q^D(P_x) by N_C to get the market demand, Q^D(P_x, P_y, I, N_C).
3. Derive individual firm supply of X, q^S(P_x), as a function of w, r, \overline{K}
using the tools of producer theory.
4. Multiply q^S(P_x) by N_F to get the market supply, Q^S(P_x, w, r, \overline{K}, N_F).
5. Set Q^D(P_x,P_y,I,N_C) = Q^S(P_x, w, r, \overline{K}, N_F) and solve for the equilibrium price, P_x^*.
6. Plug P_x^* back into either Q^D(P_x,P_y,I,N_C) or Q^S(P_x, w, r, \overline{K}, N_F) to find Q^*_x.

Shape of the LR Industry Supply Curve

Increasing-cost industry More firms mean higher input costs for everyone;
LS curve slopes upward
Constant-cost industry More firms don't affect input costs;
LS curve is horizontal
Decreasing-cost industry More firms mean lower input costs;
LS curve slopes downward

A Note on Adjustment Times

In some industries, firms might enter faster
than existing firms can acquire more capital.

In other industries, existing firms might be able to adjust capital more quickly than new firms can enter.

No hard and fast rules!

...Just be clear on what you're assuming about the "long run."
