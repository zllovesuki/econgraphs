Defining the Budget Set

A budget set is the set of all bundles available to a consumer,
given all relevant constraints

The simple case: p_xx + p_yy \le I

The Simple Budget Set

Analyzes tradeoff between two goods, X and Y,
for a consumer with income I budgeted for X and Y.

p_x Price of good X p_y Price of good Y
x Quantity of good X bought y Quantity of good Y bought
p_xx Expenditure on X p_yy Expenditure on Y

Therefore, p_xx + p_yy \le I means
"total expenditure on X, plus total expenditure on Y,
must not exceed the income budgeted to X and Y."

Constraint: P_xx + P_yy \le I

Suppose y = 0

Buy only X

P_xx + p_y \times 0 = I

\displaystyle{\Rightarrow x = \frac{I}{p_x}}

Suppose x = 0

Buy only Y

p_x \times 0 + p_yy = I

\displaystyle{\Rightarrow y = \frac{I}{p_y}}

The budget line is the set of points that exhausts the budget:

y = \frac{I}{P_y} - \frac{P_x}{P_y}x

Note the slope: -\frac{P_x}{P_y}

The budget set or feasible set is the "affordable area" with money left over.

Optimal Consumption when Calculus Works

...a deep dive into Cobb-Douglas utility functions

If these three conditions hold:
(a) The consumer's preferences over X and Y are
continuously differentiable, strictly monotonic and strictly convex
(b) The indifference curves do not cross the axes
(c) The budget constraint is a simple straight line
Then the solution to the utility-maximization problem:
1. Will involve strictly positive quantities X and Y
(an interior solution, not a corner solution)
2. Can be found using the Lagrangian method
3. Is characterized by the conditions
\displaystyle{\frac{MU_x}{MU_y} = \frac{P_x}{P_y} }       and       P_xx + P_yy = I

(i.e., is the point on the budget line that satisfies the tangency condition)

The Simple Utility Maximization Problem: General Case

You have a budget of I and can buy units of X and Y at prices P_x and P_y, respectively.

Your objective is to maximize your utility function is u(x,y) given this constraint.

Assuming "more is better," you will spend your entire income on X and Y.

In words...

Formally...

Find the maximum of the objective function
u(x,y)

\displaystyle{\max_{(x,y)}} u(x,y)

subject to the constraint
P_xx + P_yy = I.

Using the Lagrangian Method: General Case

Objective Function:
First Order Conditions:
Solve for x^* and y^*:
Set \lambda in the first two FOC's
equal to each other:
\displaystyle{\lambda = \frac{MU_x}{P_x} = \frac{MU_y}{P_y} }\displaystyle{ \Rightarrow \frac{MU_x}{MU_y} = \frac{P_x}{P_y}}

This condition, plus the budget constraint (and third FOC) P_xx + P_yy = I, make two equations in two unknowns (x and y).

We just found that in solving the Lagrangian,

\displaystyle{\lambda = \frac{MU_x}{P_x} = \frac{MU_y}{P_y}}

How should we interpret \lambda intuitively?

The change in optimal quantity of X or Y resulting from
a $1 rise in price of that good. The change in optimal quantity of X or Y resulting from
a $1 rise in income. The change in utility resulting from
a $1 rise in the price of either X or Y. The change in utility resulting from
a $1 rise in income.

Life Doesn't Have to Be Pain

Recall that for a Cobb-Douglas utility function of the form u(x,y) = cx^ay^b,
the Marginal Rate of Substitution is \frac{ay}{bx}.

Step 1.
Tangency Condition:

MRS_{x,y} = \displaystyle{\frac{P_x}{P_y}}
\displaystyle{\frac{ay}{bx}} = \displaystyle{\frac{P_x}{P_y}}
P_yy = \displaystyle{\frac{b}{a}P_xx}

Step 2.
Budget Constraint:

P_xx + \color{red}{P_yy} = I
\displaystyle{P_xx + \frac{b}{a}P_xx} = I
P_xx = \displaystyle{\left(\frac{a}{a + b}\right)I}

Step 3.
Solve for P_yy:

P_yy = \displaystyle{\frac{b}{a}P_xx}
P_yy = \displaystyle{\frac{b}{a}\left(\frac{a}{a + b}\right)I}
P_yy = \displaystyle{\left(\frac{b}{a + b}\right)I}

IMPORTANT FACT ABOUT COBB-DOUGLAS

For the Cobb-Douglas utility function u(x,y) = cx^ay^b

the consumer will spend fraction \displaystyle{\frac{a}{a+b}} of her income on X

and the remaining fraction \displaystyle{\frac{b}{a+b}} of her income on Y:

\displaystyle{x^* = \left(\frac{a}{a+b}\right)\frac{I}{P_x}}

\displaystyle{y^* = \left(\frac{b}{a+b}\right)\frac{I}{P_y}}

Summary

Object: find the point in the budget set
such that no other point is both preferred and affordable.

Approach: compare the consumer's willingness to trade off between X and Y (the MRS) with the market's willingness to trade off between them (the price ratio)

We solved for the optimal consumption choice with calculus (the Lagrangian method)

An interior solution under ideal ("regular") conditions is characterized by the tangency condition MRS_{x,y} = \frac{P_x}{P_y} and the budget constraint P_xx + P_yy = I.

In the special case of Cobb-Douglas preferences, consumers will spend a constant fraction of their income on each good, regardless of the price; and that fraction is determined by the exponents on the function.
