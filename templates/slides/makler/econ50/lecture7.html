Solving Kinked Budget Constraint Problems

Step 1:Find the utility-maximizing point on each segment of the constraint.

  • If the utility function is smooth (differentiable), solve the normal constrained optimization problem, and see if the result occurs within the segment.
  • If the utility function isn't smooth, use the logic you would otherwise use.
  • For example, perfect substitutes will tend to have a solution at a kink or a corner;
    perfect complements will involve purchasing goods in a particular proportion.

Step 2: Compare the best along each segment, to find the overall best.

  • Drawing indifference curves carefully through the kinks and corners can help!

Income and Substitution Effects

Measure the ways in which
a consumer responds to a change in price

Remember: For Interior Solutions (no kinks/corners)

Tangency Condition: MRS_{x,y} = \frac{P_x}{P_y}

Budget Line Condition: P_xx + P_yy = I

What happens when the price changes?

It changes the
price ratio...		 ...which affects the tangency condition. One good will be relatively cheaper than before the price change, the other good will be relatively more expensive.
- -
It changes your budget set... ...which affects the budget line. A decrease in price allows your income to buy more than you could before the price change; an increase in price means you would need more income to afford your original bundle.

FORMAL DEFINITIONS

The substitution effect is the change in the amount of a good that would be consumed as the price of that good changes, holding constant all other prices and the level of utility.

The income effect is the change in the amount of a good
that a consumer would buy as purchasing power changes,
holding constant all prices.

INFORMAL DEFINITIONS

Suppose the price of a good goes down.

You could now afford to be
just as happy as you were before
(move along your indifference curve)

by buying more of that good, and less of
other, now relatively more expensive goods
saving some money in the meantime.

This change in consumption would be the
substitution effect.

...but suppose you don't save the money.

You can spend that money to be
happier than you were before
(move to a higher indifference curve)

by buying more of one or both goods
(depending on whether they're normal goods or inferior goods).

This change in consumption would be the income effect.

Two New Utility Functions

Constant Elasticity of Substitution (CES) Has a parameter for substitutability.
In fact, Cobb-Douglas, perfect complements, and perfect substitutes are all special cases of this function!
- -
Inferior Good Utility Function Has a negative Income-Consumption Curve (over a relevantregion)
Even displays "Giffen Good" characteristics for high incomes!

Constant Elasticity of Substitution

The CES Utility Function: u(x,y) = \left(a x^r + by^r\right)^\frac{1}{r}

MRS_{x,y} = \frac{MU_x}{MU_y} = \frac{ax^{r-1}}{by^{r-1}}

  • The parameters a and b measure how much the consumer likes goods X and Y.
  • The parameter r \le 1 adjusts the complementarity or substitutability of the goods:
    • When r = 1, the goods are perfect substitutes: MRS = \frac{a}{b}.
    • As r \rightarrow 0 in the limit, the goods are Cobb-Douglas: MRS \rightarrow \frac{ay}{bx}.
    • As r \rightarrow -\infty in the limit, the goods are perfect complements.
    • If a = b = 1 and r = \frac{1}{2}, it becomes our "weak substitutes" example: u(x,y) = \left(\sqrt x + \sqrt y\right)^2.

PRICE-CONSUMPTION CURVES
AND SUBSTITUTABILITY

If price-consumption curves
are upward-sloping,

it means that the quantities
of goods X and Y

move in the same direction
when the price changes;

so they're complements.

If price-consumption curves
are downward-sloping,

it means that the quantities
of goods X and Y

move in opposite directions
when the price changes;

so they're substitutes.

An Inferior/Giffen Good:

u(x,y) = a \ln (x - x_\text{min}) - b \ln (y_\text{max} - y)

The x_\text{min} parameter shows that there is a "subsistence level" of good X.

The y_\text{max} parameter shows that there is a "saturation level" of good Y.

Displays negative-sloped Income-Consumption Curve nearly everywhere, and Giffen Good behavior for high levels of income.

Not a standard utility function! Violates all kinds of assumptions! DO NOT MEMORIZE!

The Slutsky Decomposition Diagram

Point Description Utility Price Income
A Initial bundle Initial utility Initial price Actual income
B "Decomposition" bundle Initial utility Final price Compensated income
C Final bundle Final utility Final price Actual income

Point A is the optimal bundle for the consumer's income with the original price of X.

Point C is the optimal bundle for the consumer's income with at the new price of X.

Point B is the "decomposition point" on the original indifference curve (U_1).

At the new price of X, the consumer could afford it with less income (BL_d).

Summary: Income and Substitution Effects

Substitution Effect: Hold utility constant, minimize cost:
Initial Point => Decomposition Point

Income Effect: Hold prices constant, increase utility:
Decomposition Point => Final Point

With substitutes, the substitution effect dominates

With complements, the income effect dominates

With inferior goods, the income effect is negative for the inferior good

With Giffen goods, the income effect is so negative
that the result of a decrease in price is to decrease consumption.
