Remember from calculus: product rule:

\frac{d}{dx}f(x)g(x) = f(x) \times \frac{dg(x)}{dx} + \frac{df(x)}{dx} \times g(x)

and by extension, if g(x) = x:

\frac{d}{dx}f(x)x = f(x) \times 1 + \frac{df(x)}{dx} \times x

Marginal Revenue and Elasticity

\displaystyle{\epsilon_{Q,P} = \frac{dQ}{dP}\frac{P}{Q}}

We can express MR in terms of P and elasticity:

\displaystyle{MR = P + \frac{dP}{dQ}Q}\displaystyle{ = P\left(1 + \frac{dP}{dQ}\frac{Q}{P}\right)}\displaystyle{ = P\left(1 + \frac{1}{\epsilon_{Q,P}}\right)}

We just showed that \displaystyle{MR = P\left(1 + \frac{1}{\epsilon_{Q,P}}\right)}.

Inverse Elasticity Pricing Rule

How much is price set above marginal cost?

INVERSE ELASTICITY PRICING RULE

Recall from before: \displaystyle{MR = P\left(1 + \frac{1}{\epsilon_{Q,P}}\right)}.

Profit maximization => set MC = MR:

\displaystyle{MC(Q^*) = P^*\left(1 + \frac{1}{\epsilon_{Q,P}}\right)}

Rearrange:

\displaystyle{\frac{P^* - MC(Q^*)}{P^*} = -\frac{1}{\epsilon_{Q,P}}}

LERNER INDEX: Quantifying Market Power

Inverse Elasticity Pricing Rule: \displaystyle{\frac{P^* - MC(Q^*)}{P^*} = -\frac{1}{\epsilon_{Q,P}}}

We don't observe demand elasticity directly;

but we can observe price markup above marginal cost!

Lerner Index: \displaystyle{\frac{P - MC}{P}}

0 for a perfectly competitive industry;

increases with market power
