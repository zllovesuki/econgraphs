Many believe that most costs in health care are fixed because so much care is delivered using shared staff, space, and equipment.

The result of this misguided thinking is that cost reduction efforts tend to focus on only the small fraction of costs seen as variable, such as drugs and supplies, which are sometimes referred to as marginal or incremental costs...

If most costs were fixed, growth in demand for health care would increase only that small fraction of costs that are variable, leading to lower average costs in the system, not the dramatically higher share of GDP now being devoted to health care.

Harvard Business Review, November 2011

Shephard's Lemma

If you know the total cost function,

you can "back out"

the conditional demand functions

for labor and capital (or any input).

For f(L,K) = L^\frac{1}{4}K^\frac{1}{4}, we found:

L^*(w,r,q) = w^{-\frac{1}{2}}r^\frac{1}{2}q^2

K^*(w,r,q) = w^\frac{1}{2}r^{-\frac{1}{2}}q^2

TC(w,r,q) = wL^* + rK^* = 2w^\frac{1}{2}r^\frac{1}{2}q^2

Note: \displaystyle{\frac{\partial TC}{\partial w} = w^{-\frac{1}{2}}r^\frac{1}{2}q^2}= L^*!

SHEPHARD'S LEMMA

.

\displaystyle{\frac{\partial TC(w,r,q)}{\partial w} = L^*(w,r,q)}

.

\displaystyle{\frac{\partial TC(w,r,q)}{\partial R} = K^*(w,r,q)}

Why does this work? (Not on test!)

.

TC(w,r,q) = wL^*(w,r,q) + rK^*(w,r,q)

.

By the chain rule:

.

\displaystyle{\frac{\partial TC(w,r,q)}{\partial w} = L^*(w,r,q) + w\frac{\partial L^*(w,r,q)}{\partial w} + r\frac{\partial K^*(w,r,q)}{\partial w}}

.

The second and third terms must sum to zero...why?

Tangency condition! \left(MRTS = \frac{w}{r}\right)

From now on, a lot of times we'll say

"We know by Shephard's lemma

that L^* is this, and K^* is that."
