What Would They Mean for Beneficiaries?

Economic theory states that if the relative prices of goods and services change, consumers will substitute relatively less expensive goods for relatively more expensive goods.
They do this in order to maximize their standard of living given their expenditure budget.

The implication of this substitution behavior is at the crux of the CPI debate: the amount of money that would be needed for these consumers to maintain the same standard of living after a price increase is less than it would be if they still purchased the same quantity of different goods (i.e., if they did not engage in substitution).

Gary Koenig & Mikki Waid, AARP Public Policy Institute

A New/Old Tool: Cost Minimization

For a given level of utility U ,

and a given set of prices (P_x,P_y) ,

what is the least costly bundle (x^*,y^*)

that achieves the desired utility?

The Dual Problem: Two Equivalent Ways to Optimize

Utility Maximization

Cost Minimization

\max_{x,y} u(x,y)
s.t. P_xx + P_yy = I
\min_{x,y} P_xx + P_yy
s.t. u(x,y) = U

Solve for x^* and y^*; the solutions are:

Marshallian Demand Functions:

x^*(P_x,P_y,I),y^*(P_x,P_y,I)

Hicksian Demand Functions:

x^*(U,P_x,P_y), y^*(U,P_x,P_y)

Plug x^* and y^* back into the objective function:

Indirect Utility Function:

V(P_x,P_y,I) = u[x^*(P_x,P_y,I),y^*(P_x,P_y,I)]

(Utility from utility-maximizing choice)

Expenditure Function:

E(U,P_x,P_y) = P_xx^*(U,P_x,P_y) + P_yy^*(U, P_x,P_y)

(Cost of cost-minimizing choice)

How to Derive Hicksian Demand from Marshallian Demand

Example: Cobb-Douglas u(x,y) = xy

Start with Marshallian demand:
\displaystyle{x^* = \frac{I}{2P_x}, y^* = \frac{I}{2P_y}}
Plug (x^*,y^*) back into u(x,y) = xy
to find the indirect utility function:
\displaystyle{u(x^*,y^*) = \left(\frac{I}{2P_x}\right)\left(\frac{I}{2P_y}\right) } \displaystyle{\Rightarrow V(P_x,P_y,I) = \frac{I^2}{4P_xP_y} }
Set the indirect utility function equal to U and solve for Ito find the expenditure function:
\displaystyle{\color{green}{\frac{I^2}{4P_xP_y}} = U} \Rightarrow I^2 = 4P_xP_yU \displaystyle{\Rightarrow E(P_x,P_y,U) = 2P_x^\frac{1}{2}P_y^\frac{1}{2}U^\frac{1}{2}}
Plug this "lowest cost" back into the Marshallian demand (as the income) to get the Hicksian demand:
\displaystyle{x^* = \frac{\color{blue}{E(P_x,P_y,U)}}{2P_x} = \frac{2P_x^\frac{1}{2}P_y^\frac{1}{2}U^\frac{1}{2}}{2P_x}} \displaystyle{\Rightarrow x^H(P_x,P_y,U) = \left(\frac{P_y}{P_x}\right)^\frac{1}{2}U^\frac{1}{2}}

MAGNITUDE OF CS, CV, AND EV

In general:

For a price drop, CV < CS < EV

For a price increase, CV > CS > EV

Special Case: Quasilinear Utility => CV = CS = EV

SUMMARY

Welfare analysis tries to quantify the harm/good done to people
by some change (in this case, price).

By analyzing the change in income required to keep someone at the same level of utility,
we avoid having to quantify utility changes.

We saw several new mathematical constructs:
compensated demand, indirect utility functions, and expenditure functions.
These are all just different ways of parsing the same optimization problem.

Looking forward: we're going to use this cost minimization approach
a lot when we get to producer theory.
