Consumer Preferences: Vocabulary and Notation

We call the objects of consumer choice bundles or baskets.

Given a choice between bundle A and bundle B, we say that:

Symbols Meaning Informally quantitative interpretation
A \succ B A is strictly preferred to B The consumer likes A more than B.
A \succeq B A is weakly preferred to B The consumer likes A at least as much as B.
A \sim B Indifferent between A and B The consumer likes A and B the same amount.
A \preceq B A is weakly dispreferred to B The consumer likes A at most as much as B.
A \prec B A is strictly dispreferred to B The consumer likes A less than B.

Consumer Preferences: Fundamental Assumptions

Preferences are complete. Any two bundles can be compared. For any A and B,
either A \succeq B or B \succeq A.
Preferences are transitive. If you like A at least as much as B,
and you like B at least as much as C,
then you like A at least as much as C.		 If A \succeq B and B \succeq C,
then A \succeq C.

Together, these assumptions mean that consumers can rank all possible consumption bundles in a coherent way.

How would you rank these bundles?

Bundle A is preferred to bundle B (that is, A \succ B)

Indifference curves

The Indifference Curve for Bundle A:

(1) Shows all the bundles the consumer likes just as much as A (is "indifferent" between that bundle and A).

(2) Partitions the entire choice space into bundles which are preferred to A,
and those which are dispreferred to A.

{% raw %}

A \succ B
since B lies below
the indifference curve passing through A.

A \prec B
since B lies above
the indifference curve passing through A.

A \sim B
since B lies along
the indifference curve passing through A.

Possible Assumption 2: Convexity (“Variety is Better”)

Bundle C is preferred to bundles A and B
...that is, C \succ A and C \succ B
if A and B are on the same indifference curve
...that is, A \sim B
and C is a convex combination of A and B.

...that is, the quantities in bundle C are weighted averages of those in A and B.

Monotonicity & Convexity Together

If an agent's preferences

are monotonic (more is better)

and convex (variety is better),

then indifference curves

will be downward-sloping

and bowed in to the origin.

(Spoiler alert: think about the function f(x,y) = xy,

which has level curves that look like y = \frac{k}{x}.)

Diminishing MRS

The twin assumptions of monotonicity and convexity

also give us diminishing MRS,

which is related to diminishing marginal returns.

Possible Assumption 3: Smoothness

We have begun to measure preferences using the notion of the Marginal Rate of Substitution (MRS).
