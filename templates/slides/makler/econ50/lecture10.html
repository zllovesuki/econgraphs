Returns to scale

Suppose that if you use

L units of labor and K units of capital

you produce q = f(L,K) units of output.

If you use tL and tK for some t > 1,

is the amount you produce - that is, f(tL,tK) -

more or less than tq units of output?

(Colloquially: "If you double all inputs,
do you double your output, or produce more, or less?")

RETURNS TO SCALE: DEFINITIONS

Decreasing
returns to scale		 f(tL,tK) < tf(L,K) "Doubling all inputs produces
less than double the output."
Constant
returns to scale		 f(tL,tK) = tf(L,K) "Doubling all inputs produces
exactly double the output."
Increasing
returns to scale		 f(tL,tK) > tf(L,K) "Doubling all inputs produces
more than double the output."

Group Exercise: Production Technology

Enter the results in the spreadsheet at http://tinyurl.com/econ50googledocs;
click on "Lecture 10 Group Activities".

You have a firm with production technology q = f(L,K) = L^\alpha K^\frac{1}{2}.

  1. Let \alpha be the fraction of your group that is men.
  2. Let K be the number of women in your group.
  3. Calculate your output for L = 10, L = 20, and L = 30.
  4. Calculate your MP_L for L = 10, L = 20, and L = 30.
  5. Is the MP_L diminishing, constant, or increasing?
  6. Now calculate your output for L = 20 and you double your capital. Compare this to the case where L = 10 with your original level of capital. Does your firm have constant, increasing, or decreasing returns to scale?
  7. Use desmos.com to draw an isoquant for Q = 100 with your original level of capital. Now add another isoquant for Q = 100 if you double your value of \alpha. What happens?
